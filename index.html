The GRC Engineering Cheat Sheet

For decades, auditors and governments defined and molded Legacy GRC in their image. Today, engineers and analysts are transforming it into something new: GRC Engineering. This cheat sheet outlines what makes GRC Engineering different.

GRC

Governance, risk, and compliance (GRC) are three related facets that aim to assure an organization reliably achieves objectives, addresses uncertainty and acts with integrity. Wikipedia

Engineering

Engineering is the practice of using natural science, mathematics, and the engineering design process to solve problems within technology, increase efficiency and productivity, and improve systems. Wikipedia

Terms

General

  • Systems Thinking An analytical approach that examines how components of a system interrelate and work together over time within larger systems. In GRC Engineering, applied across organizational governance, risk analysis, and control modeling to understand emergent behavior rather than treating each element in isolation.
  • Design Thinking A human-centered problem-solving methodology that emphasizes empathy, ideation, and iteration. In GRC Engineering, harnessed to make the right thing to do the easy thing to do—designing controls and processes that people naturally follow rather than work around.
  • Threat-Informed Grounding GRC activities—policies, controls, trainings, risk assessments—in real-world threat intelligence rather than abstract framework checklists. Ensures that defenses address actual adversary behaviors and attack patterns, not just theoretical compliance gaps.
  • GRC as a Product Treating GRC programs as products that serve internal and external customers, with user research, feedback loops, iterative improvement, and measurable outcomes—replacing the legacy model where GRC exists as an internal program serving primarily the GRC team’s own needs.

Governance

  • Policy-as-Code Policies written and maintained directly as executable code (e.g., OPA Rego, Sentinel). The policy source of truth is the code itself, enabling version control, automated testing, and deterministic enforcement at deploy time or runtime.
  • Policy-to-Code The process of translating human-readable policy documents into executable code. Starts from natural-language policies and produces corresponding code representations, bridging the gap between policy authors and enforcement systems.
  • Policy-from-Code Deriving or generating human-readable policy documentation from existing code, configurations, or runtime behavior. Ensures that written policies reflect what is actually enforced, closing the gap between documentation and control reality.
  • PaC Policy as Code. Shorthand for encoding governance policies into executable code that can be evaluated automatically—for example, enforcing risk tolerance thresholds before a deployment or change is approved.
  • Risk Tolerance The level of risk an organization is willing to accept in pursuit of its objectives. In GRC Engineering, risk tolerance is codified and enforced programmatically (via PaC) at pre-deploy or pre-change gates, rather than expressed only in narrative policy documents.
  • Real-time Behavioral Interventions Context-aware nudges or guardrails delivered to employees at the moment a risky or non-compliant action is about to occur, replacing static annual or semi-annual training with continuous, in-the-flow guidance.
  • Scientific Pedagogy Applying evidence-based learning science—spaced repetition, scenario-based exercises, measurable retention—to security and compliance training, replacing checkbox-style annual courses with methods proven to change behavior.
  • Metrics-focused Committees Governance bodies that base decisions on quantitative data—risk metrics, control effectiveness rates, incident trends—rather than relying on subjective discussion or status-quo reporting without measurable outcomes.

Risk

  • TPCM Third-party compliance management. Legacy GRC approach to third-party “risk” management. Primarily focused on whether or not third parties comply with a customer’s requirements as expressed in contract language and questionnaires. Does not account for real-world threats, value-at-risk, or positive vs. negative risk tradeoffs. Conflates outside-in vulnerability scanning with risk analysis.
  • TPRM Third-party risk management. The GRC Engineering counterpart to TPCM. Balances third-party and first-party risk with equal rigor, evaluating real-world threat scenarios, value-at-risk, and risk tradeoffs rather than reducing the discipline to questionnaire-based compliance checking alone.
  • Qualitative Risk Analysis Assessing risk using subjective scales (e.g., High / Medium / Low) based on expert judgment. The legacy GRC default—manual, inconsistent across assessors, and difficult to aggregate or compare meaningfully.
  • Quantitative Risk Analysis Assessing risk using numerical models, probability distributions, and measurable data (e.g., annualized loss expectancy, Monte Carlo simulations). The GRC Engineering approach—automated, reproducible, and comparable across risk scenarios.
  • Heatmaps Color-coded matrices plotting likelihood against impact on ordinal scales. A hallmark of legacy GRC risk reporting. Often criticized for obscuring actual risk magnitude behind coarse, subjective categories that resist meaningful comparison.
  • Histograms Frequency-distribution charts showing the spread of possible loss outcomes from quantitative risk analysis. Replace heatmaps in GRC Engineering by conveying the shape, range, and confidence intervals of risk in objective, data-driven terms.
  • Risk Scenarios Holistic descriptions of risk that combine a threat, attack vector, affected asset, and resulting impact into a single, analyzable unit. GRC Engineering uses these in place of fragmented weakness or issue lists to model risk realistically and prioritize response.
  • FUD Fear, Uncertainty, and Doubt. A persuasion tactic common in legacy GRC where risk is communicated through alarming narratives rather than objective evidence, often to justify budget or authority without rigorous analysis.
  • ELMR Evidence, Logic, Math, Reason. The GRC Engineering alternative to FUD. Risk decisions and communications are grounded in verifiable data, sound logic, mathematical models, and reasoned argument rather than fear-based rhetoric.
  • Decision Support The GRC Engineering posture toward the business: acting as partners who provide data, analysis, and options so stakeholders can make informed risk decisions—replacing the legacy “accountability police” model of enforcement without context.

Compliance

  • Control Monitoring The practice of observing whether security and compliance controls are operating as intended. Legacy GRC performs this periodically and in isolation; GRC Engineering automates it continuously and holistically across the control environment.
  • Active Testing Proactively exercising controls to confirm they function correctly—not just checking that they exist. A GRC Engineering complement to passive monitoring, analogous to how software engineers write automated tests to verify system behavior.
  • Evidence Samples A subset of records selected to demonstrate that a control operated during an audit period. The legacy GRC norm—limited by manual collection effort, inherently incomplete, and vulnerable to selection bias.
  • Evidence Populations The complete set of records for a control over a given period, collected and retained automatically. The GRC Engineering standard—eliminates sampling risk by providing full coverage and enabling statistical analysis of control effectiveness.

Trust & Assurance

  • RFIs Requests for Information. Inquiries from customers, prospects, or partners asking an organization to demonstrate its security and compliance posture. Legacy GRC handles these manually via email; GRC Engineering enables self-service completion and real-time responses.
  • Self-service RFIs A GRC Engineering model where customers and prospects can answer their own security questions through portals, trust centers, or automated questionnaire engines—eliminating the manual email back-and-forth of legacy GRC.
  • Real-time Visibility Providing transparent, up-to-date, and historically browsable views into an organization’s control status. Replaces the legacy model of opaque, abstracted annual artifacts (e.g., SOC 2 reports shared once a year) with continuous assurance.
